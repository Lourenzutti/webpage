Hypothesis Testing

So far, we have focused on the problem of estimation. We have a population of interest, and we are interested in the specific value of an unknown parameter of this population (e.g., the population mean, median, quantiles, standard deviation). However, oftentimes we are not interested in the value of the parameter per se, but we are just interested in whether a certain statement about the population is true.

During the summer, Vancouver's beaches get pretty busy. To ensure everybody's safety, the city of Vancouver monitors if the beaches' waters are safe for swimming. To test the water quality of a beach, the city of Vancouver takes multiple samples of the water and calculates the average number of E. coli (a bacteria) per \(100\)mL. If the average number of E. coli per \(100\)mL is less than \(400\), the city considers the beach proper for swimming. Otherwise, the city of Vancouver releases a warning to let people know that they should not swim on the beach. In this case, the actual precise number of E. coli per \(100\)mL is of less interest. Instead, the focus is to determine whether the water is safe, i.e., if the number of E. coli per \(100\)mL is less than \(400\).

We have two possible scenarios in this case: (1) the water is unsafe for swimming; or (2) the water is safe for swimming.

Different people run at different speeds. Do men run faster than women, on average? In this case, we are comparing two populations: males and females. But our main interest is not obtaining an accurate estimate of the difference in average speed between the populations. Instead, it is to check if there is a difference at all (large or small). We have two possible scenarios, either the men and women run at the same average speed, or they don't.

Some people infected by a disease receive a medical treatment and die. Other, receive a medical treatment and live. Does the treatment increase the chance of survival? Again, our focus here is not to determine precisely the increase in the chances of survival caused by the treatment. Instead, we just want to check if there's an increase at all. In other words, we are trying to determine whether the treatment helps, not how much it helps. We have two possible scenarios, either the treatment helps or doesn't.

Statistical Hypotheses

Generally, in hypothesis testing, we have two possible scenarios (no more, no less). Note that the scenarios refer to the population, and each scenario is called a hypothesis.

Statistical Hypothesis

A statistical hypothesis is a statement about the population distribution(s).

Note that Definition 1.1 does not specify what kind of statement we are making about the population distribution. It can be a statement about the functional form of the distribution, such as the population distribution is symmetric; or the population is normally distributed. But it can be more specific, a statement about a population parameter, such as the population mean is higher than 10; the population variance is equal to 1; the mean between two populations is equal. Here we will focus on the second case, and our hypothesis will be about population parameters.

Since we always have exactly two scenarios in classical statistical hypothesis testing, we have exactly two hypotheses. They are commonly referred to as the Null Hypothesis (denoted by \(H_0\)) and the Alternative Hypothesis (denoted by \(H_A\) or \(H_1\)). The null hypothesis refers to the status quo; it is the hypothesis of no change. The alternative hypothesis is usually the hypothesis that something has changed; something interesting happened. In practice, you can think of the null hypothesis as the hypothesis that we will reject if and only if there is "strong enough evidence" against it. If we still have "reasonable doubt" that \(H_0\) could be true, we will not reject it. For this reason, we say that the null hypothesis is the status quo. The alternative hypothesis, on the other hand, is the complement of the null hypothesis, and it is true if and only if \(H_0\) is false. Again, there is no other possible scenario besides the null and alternative hypotheses.

The city of Vancouver considers the beaches safe for swimming if the water has fewer than 400 E. Coli bacterias per 100mL of water. So, the City of Vancouver wants to test whether the average number of E. Coli per 100mL, \(\mu\), is less than 400. $$\left\{ \begin{align} H_0: & \quad \mu \geq 400\\ H_1: & \quad \mu \lt 400 \end{align}\right.$$ Note that, by setting the hypothesis this way, the city's default behaviour is to release a warning about the safety of the water, unless there is "strong" evidence that the water is safe to swimming. Alternatively, the city of Vancouver could take the instance that all beaches are safe for swimming, unless there is "strong" evidence otherwise. In this case, the hypotheses would be: $$\left\{ \begin{align} H_0: & \quad \mu \leq 400\\ H_1: & \quad \mu \gt 400 \end{align}\right.$$ Remember, \(H_0\) is the status quo hypothesis, the default conclusion if the evidence is not "strong" enough.

Do men run faster than women, on average? In this case, we are comparing two populations: males and females. Unless there is "strong" evidence that men run faster than women, we are going to assume they do not. What we want to test in this case is if both populations have the same average. In other words, $$\left\{ \begin{align} H_0: & \quad \mu_{\text{man}} = \mu_{\text{women}}\\ H_1: & \quad \mu_{\text{man}} > \mu_{\text{women}} \end{align}\right.$$

Unless we see "strong" evidence that a new untested drug works, we will assume it does not. In this case: $$\left\{ \begin{align} H_0: & \quad\text{the new drug does not work}\\ H_1: & \quad\text{the new drug works} \end{align}\right.$$ But wait for a second, we said that a statistical hypothesis is a statement about population distribution(s). What are the population(s) in this case? What are \(H_0\) and \(H_1\) referring to? In this case, we have two abstract populations: (1) the population of all sick patients who would potentially receive the drug; and (2) the population of all sick patients who would potentially not receive the drug. Finally, how can we say that the drug works? That will depend on how we measure its efficacy. One could for example, see if the mortality rate of patients who received the drug, \(p_2\) is significantly lower (we'll discuss what this means soon) than the mortality rate of patients who did not receive the drug, \(p_1\). Then, the hypotheses would be: $$\left\{ \begin{align} H_0: & \quad p_1 = p_2\\ H_1: & \quad p_1 > p_2 \end{align}\right.$$ Or, if the drug is to treat pain, one could check the pain of patients before and after they take the drug. Then, we check if average pain after the patients take the drug, \(\mu_1\), lower than before they take the drug, \(\mu_2\). In this case, we would have: $$\left\{ \begin{align} H_0: & \quad \mu_1 = \mu_2\\ H_1: & \quad \mu_1 > \mu_2 \end{align}\right.$$ Note, however, that concluding if a drug is effective is not just a matter of comparing two populations. One needs to carefully design the experiment from which the data will be collected so that a causal conclusion that patients improved because of the drug, and not for any other hidden reason, can be made.

Hypotheses and the parametric space

As we have seen earlier, the two hypotheses in a hypothesis test must cover all possible scenarios without having anything in common. This means that one and only one of the hypotheses is true. Now, suppose we are interested in the median of a population. What are the possible values for this parameter? In general, the median can be any number, positive, negative, or zero. In some cases, however, we can restrict the possible values of the median because we have extra information about the problem. For example, the variable of interest is the red blood cell count in 1 microliter of blood; we already know that the median cannot be negative. Similar reasoning applies to other parameters as well. For example, one might be interested in the population's standard deviation, which is known to be nonnegative. However, one might have some extra information on the problem and can restrict the possible values even more. They could know that the standard deviation is higher than a 10, for example. This notion of what possible values a parameter can take is important to model our uncertainty.

Parametric Space

The parametric space of a parameter \(\theta\) is the set of all possible values that \(\theta\) can take.

Note that \(\theta\) is just a symbol commonly used to refer to an arbitrary parameter (which could be mean, or median, or a quantile, or the std. deviation, or anything really). When we are testing a parameter of a population distribution, the hypotheses split the parametric space into two subsets that share no values in common and together contain all values in the parametric space.

In Example 1.1, the city of Vancouver wanted to conduct the following hypothesis test: $$\left\{ \begin{align} H_0: & \quad \mu \geq 400\\ H_1: & \quad \mu \lt 400 \end{align}\right.$$ This means that, for the city of Vancouver, the average count of E. Coli bacteria in 100mL of water can be anything less than 400, could be 400, or anything above 400. Therefore, these hypotheses together form a parametric space that contains any number. In other words, the city of Vancouver is not restricting the parametric space based on any other information. Naturally, in this case, \(\mu\) cannot be negative since it is the average number of E. Coli in 100mL of water. So, we could say that the parametric space here is any nonnegative number (we could technically represent this by making \(H_1: \ \ 0\leq\mu\leq 400\), but doing so is quite unusual).

A much more common way to set up the hypotheses in introduction to statistics books is: $$\left\{ \begin{align} H_0: & \quad \mu = 400\\ H_1: & \quad \mu \lt 400 \end{align}\right.$$ In this case, the combined hypotheses form a parametric space that contains any value between 0 (inclusive) and 400 (inclusive). This setup is used even if we are not sure that the mean \(\mu\) could not be higher than 400. The reason for using equality instead of \(\geq\) is that it simplifies the problem and, in most practical cases, does not affect the analysis. I'll make another comment on this later on.
