Sampling Distribution

This year, 2020, there will be a presidential election in the United States. Every election year we see a bunch of surveys trying to draw a picture on how people are going to vote. But how do they make these surveys? Do these polls check how all the people are going to vote? According to this table from US Census Bureau there are approximately 233.5 million voting-age citizens. It would certainly require a lot of resources, to ask all 233.5 millions voting-age American citizens.

Map by Free Vector Maps: http://freevectormaps.com

To make things more complicated, the political scenario changes quite fast. Therefore, time is also an issue. Even if you had the resources to ask everyone, many political events that swing voters' opinions could happen during your survey, and by the time you are finished, your results are already outdated.

So, how can we learn (at least approximately) how voters are going to vote without asking every voter?

A natural answer for this question is: let us ask just a small portion of the voters how they are going to vote. This approach does provide us with a solution, but also many problems:

  1. How do we choose the voters to answer the survey?

  2. How many voters should answer the survey?

  3. How do we infer the knowledge obtained from the Sample to the entire population of voters?

  4. Different group of voters will have different proportion of votes.

In this chapter, we are going to focus on points 2 and 3. We will discuss points 1 and 2 in more detail later. Let's formalize some intuitive concepts.

Population and parameters

Definition: Target Population

The group of all items/people that we are interested in.

In general, we are interested in one or more unknown quantities that describe the population, like summary quantities. These quantities describe something about the entire population (not about specific individuals in the population), and they are called parameters.

Definition: Parameter

A summary quantity of the population that is unknown.

Parameters are unknown, we are trying to learn them because they tell us information about the population. Let's see an example:

Example 1.1: In the election example above, the group of all American voters is our population. Note that the population is not the group of all Americans. It is the group of American voters. We are interested in the proportion of voters that will vote for each candidate, and the proportion of voters that will abstain from voting. In this case, we have more than one parameter of interest.

Caution

A common mistake many students make is to mix up a variable of interest with a parameter of interest. For example, one might be interested in the average dolphin weight, which is a parameter; the average weight is a quantity of the population, not of a single dolphin. What is being measured, however, is the dolphin weight, which would be the variable of interest.

As discussed before, in many cases we are not able to measure the variable of interest in the entire population. In these cases, we draw a sample of the population. Let's define sample.

Sample

We are going to call sample any subset (sub-group of elements) of the population. What we hope for is that this subset would represent the population well, but this is not always the case. Even if the population is poorly represented by a sub-group of elements, such sub-group is still called a sample of the population.

Sample

A subset of the population.

It is like when you are cooking. Imagine you're making a soup and want to know if the soup has enough salt. Then, we usually just taste a spoon of the soup (a sample), and if that portion that you tasted is lacking salt, you conclude that the whole soup is lacking salt (inference - from the sample to the population). But imagine you're cooking a less waterly thing, say, french fries. Then, you take one piece to see if you have put enough salt. But, just by chance, you ended with a piece that got too much salt on top of it, and you conclude wrongly that the whole basket of french fries is salty. In this case, you got a sample that doesn't represent the population well. But again, it's still a sample.

There are many different strategies to draw a sample. One of the most common strategy is called random sampling without replacement. To take a random sampling without replacement of size n elements from a population you must:

  1. List all the elements of the population.

  2. Select one element at random from the population (all elements must have the same probability of being selected).

  3. Save the selected element and remove it from the population.

  4. Repeat Steps 2. and 3. n times.

Although random sampling without replacement is one of the simplest way to draw a sample, it is still quite effective. However, in some cases it is not the best strategy you could use. But let's not worry about this now.

Now that we have the concept of sample, we can revisit the concept of population. As it was mentioned, a sample is a subset of the population. But frequently the population that the sample is drawn from is not the same population we are interested in. Let us see an example.

Example 2.1: In times of Covid-19, the Canadian government started providing some aid to Canadian workers that lost their jobs. The provincial government of British Columbia (BC) is studying if it should also provide some aid to university students. To make the decision, BC's government wants to know the average income of university students in the province. There are many universities in BC, and sampling students from all universities can be costly and time-consuming. The government decides to sample students only from the University of British Columbia (UBC). By doing this, the sample that the government will be taking, comes from the population of all UBC students, which is a more restricted group than the group of all university students in BC.

Example 2.1 shows us that we need to improve our definition of population and make a distinction between the two populations.

Sampled Population

The group of all items/people which we are taking a sample from.

Ideally, the Sampled Population will coincide with the Target Population. If not, be aware that the results based on the sample can only be extrapolated (by means of statistical inference) to the Sampled Population. To extrapolate from the Sampled Population to the Target Population you need a justification beyond statistical inference -- which could be valid, but could be misleading and wrong. Careful!

It is also worth mentioning that in some cases the target population and sample population differ for reasons other than by convenience. This is very common in medical research (see the exercise below).

Great! We now know some of the basic concepts we need. Let's review the big picture.

Revisiting the Big Picture

  1. We are interested in a specific quantity (parameter) of a given population of objects/people. For example, the average income of all UBC students.

  2. Due to limited resources we cannot survey the whole population to obtain the exact value of the parameter;

  3. We then take a sample at random from the population.

  4. Next, we use the sample to learn about the parameter of the population. For example, if we are interested in the average income of a given population, we calculate the average income in the sample. The value you obtain is called estimate of the parameter.

Great, we are done! But wait a second, the sample is random. The fact that we are using Chen, Mary, and Amy's average income it is just by chance. It could have been Ronald, Mary, and Lien, or Jean, Wang, and Adebola, or any three other UBC students. Different samples would give different results.

You might even end up with a sample that is not representative of the population. For example, imagine you select three unemployed students. The average income in your sample would be smaller than the average income of all UBC's students, since not all UBC's students are unemployed.

How can we rely on this approach if every time we take a sample we get a different estimate?

Admittedly, since the estimate is a random value, we will not hit the mark and get an estimate that exactly matches the value of the parameter of interest. However, we don't need to. We just need to obtain an estimate that is "close enough most of the time".

Sampling distribution

When try to learn about an unknown parameter, taking a sample and getting an estimate is a very reasonable approach. However, different samples will provide different estimates bringing uncertainty to the results. Think about, without knowing the population, how can you know that the sample you got represents well the population and will yield a good estimate for the parameter?

In an attempt of understanding better the variation of the estimates caused by random sampling, we could the value we would obtain in each and all possible samples of a given size. For example, considering all possible samples of 3 UBC students, how many samples provides a sample average income between C$ 0.00 and C$ 50.00, between C$ 50.00 and C$ 100.00, and so on? In other words, we could consider the sample mean of all possible samples, and study its distribution.

So, how many voters should we ask (sample size)? But how is this possible? Also, what do I mean by approximately? We see that some of these polls are accurate and others are quite wrong. We will see that even the surveys with bad results are bounded to occur occasionally, and this is anticipated. Which brings us another question: what do I mean by occasionally? How frequent is that?   • What should be our sample size?
  • What do we mean by approximately?
  • What do we mean by occasionaly?
Statistical inference is the area of statistics that is focused on the extrapolation of results observed in a sample of the population to the whole population. It helps us to answer the questions above by measuring the intrinsic uncertainty and errors caused by the use of a sample of items/people instead of the whole population.
