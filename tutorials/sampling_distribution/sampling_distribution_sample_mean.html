Sampling distribution

This year, 2020, there will be a presidential election in the United States. Every election year we see a bunch of surveys trying to draw a picture on how people are going to vote. But how do they make these surveys? Do these polls check how all the people are going to vote? According to this table from US Census Bureau there are approximately 233.5 million voting-age citizens. It would certainly require a lot of resources, to ask all 233.5 millions voting-age American citizens.

Map by Free Vector Maps: http://freevectormaps.com


To make things more complicated, the political scenario changes quite fast. Therefore, time is also an issue. Even if you had the resources to ask everyone, many political events that swing voters' opinions could happen during your survey, and by the time you are finished, your results are already outdated.

So, how can we learn (at least approximately) how voters are going to vote without asking every voter?

We could ask just a small portion of the voters, how they are going to vote. Before we dive into how this works, let's formalize some intuitive concepts.

1. Population and parameters

By population we mean the group of all items/people that we are interested in. In general, we are interest in one or more parameters of the population.

A parameter is a quantity of the population that we are interested in.â€‹ It describes something about the population. The parameters are usually unknown. We are trying to learn about them. Let's see a few examples:

Example 1.1: In the election example above, the group of all American voters is our population. Note that the population is not the group of all Americans. It is the group of American voters. We are interested in the proportion of voters that will vote for each candidate, and the proportion of voters that will abstain from voting. In this case, we have more than one parameter of interest.

As discussed before, in many cases we are not able to measure the variable of interest in the entire population. In these cases, we draw a sample of the population. Let's define sample.

2. Sample

We are going to call sample any subset (sub-group of elements) of the population. What we hope for is that this subset would represent the population well, but this is not always the case. Even if the population is poorly represented by a sub-group of elements, such sub-group is still called a sample of the population.

It is like when you are cooking. Imagine you're making a soup and want to know if the soup has enough salt. Then, we usually just taste a spoon of the soup (a sample), and if that portion that you tasted is lacking salt, you conclude that the whole soup is lacking salt (inference - from the sample to the population). But imagine you're cooking a less waterly thing, say, french fries. Then, you take one french fries to see if you have put enough salt. But, just by chance, you ended with a piece that got too much salt on top of it, and you conclude wrongly that the whole basket of french fries is salty. In this case, you got a sample that doesn't represent the population well. But again, it's still a sample.

There are many different strategies to draw a sample. One of the most important is called random sampling (or sampling with replacement). To take a random sample of n elements from a population you must:

  1. List all the elements of the population.
  2. Select one element at random from the population. All elements must have the same probability of being selected.
  3. Save the selected element and put it back in the population.
  4. Repeat Steps 2. and 3. n times.
Random sampling is very mathematically convenient. However, in many cases it is not the best strategy you could use. But let's not worry about this now.

Now that we have the concept of sample, we can revisit the concept of population. As it was mentioned, a sample is a subset of the population. But frequently the population that the sample is drawn from is not the same population we are interested in. Let me try to give an example.

Example 2.1: In times of Covid-19, the Canadian government started providing some aid to Canadian workers that lost their jobs. The provincial government of British Columbia (BC) is studying if it should also provide some aid to university students. To make the decision, BC's government wants to know the average income of university students in the province. There are many universities in BC, and sampling students from all universities can be costly and time-consuming. The government decides to sample students only of the biggest university in the province, which is the University of British Columbia (UBC). By doing this the sample that the government will be taking, comes from the population of all UBC students, which is a more restricted group than the group of all university students in BC.

Example 2.1 shows us that we need to improve our definition of population and make a distinction between the two populations.

  • Target Population: The group of all items/people we are interested in.
  • Sampled Population: The group of all items/people that we actually taking our sample from.

Ideally, the Sampled Population will coincide with the Target Population. If not, be aware that your results based on the sample can only be extrapolated (by means of statistical inference) to the Sampled Population. To extrapolate from the Sample Population to the Targe Population you need a justification beyond statistical inference -- which might be valid, but might also be misleading and wrong. Careful!

It is also worht mentioning that sometimes the target population and sample population differ for reasons other than by choice. This is very common in medical research (see the exercise below).

Statistical inference is the area of statistics that is focused on the extrapolation of results observed in a sample of the population to the whole population. It helps us to answer the questions above by measuring the intrinsic uncertainty and errors caused by the use of a sample of items/people instead of the whole population.
